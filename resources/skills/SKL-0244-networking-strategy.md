## Core Concepts

Effective networking is not about collecting contacts. It is about building genuine relationships that compound over time. The best networkers give before they ask, follow up consistently, and show up where their people already gather.

### The Give-First Framework

Lead every interaction with value. Share a relevant article, make an introduction, or offer feedback on someone's work before you ever ask for anything. Cal.com's open-source scheduling model demonstrates this principle at scale: by giving away booking infrastructure freely, they built a contributor network of thousands before monetizing. Apply the same logic to your personal network. Be useful first.

### Follow-Up Systems

The real networking happens after the conversation ends. Within 48 hours of meeting someone, send a short personalized message referencing something specific from your conversation. Use a simple tracking system (spreadsheet, CRM, or notes app) with three columns: name, context of meeting, and next follow-up date. Schedule quarterly check-ins with your top 20 connections. Tools like Cal.com show that removing friction from scheduling (shared booking links, timezone handling) dramatically increases follow-through rates.

### Online and Offline Mix

Online networking (LinkedIn, Twitter/X, community forums) builds breadth. Offline networking (conferences, meetups, coffee chats) builds depth. The strongest networks combine both. Post consistently in online communities where your audience gathers, then convert online connections to real conversations. Aim for a 70/30 split: 70% online engagement to maintain presence, 30% offline to deepen trust.

### Choosing Your Channels

Not every platform deserves your time. Pick two to three channels maximum and go deep rather than spreading thin. Consider where your ideal connections already spend time. For technical professionals, that might be GitHub, dev communities, and local meetups. For business professionals, LinkedIn and industry conferences. For creators, Twitter/X and niche Slack or Discord groups.

### The Warm Introduction

Cold outreach has a response rate below 5%. Warm introductions through mutual connections convert at 10 to 20 times that rate. Before reaching out cold, check if you share a connection who can introduce you. When you do reach out cold, reference shared context (same event, same community, commented on their work) to create warmth.

## Key Takeaways

- Give value before asking for anything. Lead with generosity and the network compounds naturally.
- Build a follow-up system with names, context, and next-action dates. Consistency beats intensity.
- Combine online presence for breadth with offline conversations for depth.
- Focus on two to three channels where your target connections already gather.
- Pursue warm introductions over cold outreach whenever possible.
