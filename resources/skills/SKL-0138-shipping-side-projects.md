## Purpose

Most side projects die in the "self-entertainment stage": initial enthusiasm, no users, abandonment. This fragment provides a practical path from idea through launch to first feedback, optimized for solo builders and small teams.

---

## Phase 1: Validate Before Building

Do not spend weeks building something nobody wants. Before writing code:

1. **Describe the problem in one sentence.** If you cannot, the idea is not clear enough yet.
2. **Find 5 people who have this problem.** Talk to them. Ask what they currently do about it. If nobody cares, pivot.
3. **Check for existing solutions.** If competitors exist, that validates demand. Study what they do poorly.
4. **Define your unfair advantage.** Why will you solve this better? (Personal experience, unique audience access, technical insight.)

---

## Phase 2: Scope Ruthlessly

The number one reason side projects never ship is scope creep.

- **Pick one core action.** What is the single thing a user does? Build only that.
- **Set a time constraint.** "I will ship something in 2 weeks." Deadlines force prioritization.
- **Cut features aggressively.** If a feature is not required for the core action, it goes on the "after launch" list.
- **Use boring technology.** Pick tools you already know. Side projects fail from abandonment, not from using the wrong framework.

---

## Phase 3: Build and Show Progress

- **Build in public.** Share progress updates on social media or a blog. This creates accountability and attracts early users before launch.
- **Get something working end-to-end first.** A complete ugly flow beats a polished incomplete one. Vertical slices over horizontal layers.
- **Deploy early.** Get it on a real URL as soon as possible. Sharing a link is more powerful than describing an idea.

---

## Phase 4: Launch Day

Launch is not a single event. It is a series of posts across different platforms.

| Platform | When | Notes |
|----------|------|-------|
| Your own audience (Twitter, newsletter) | Day 1 | Warmest audience, highest conversion |
| Product Hunt | Day 1-2 | Best on Tuesday-Thursday, prepare assets in advance |
| Hacker News (Show HN) | Day 1-3 | Keep the title factual, comment with context |
| Relevant subreddits | Week 1 | Follow each community's self-promotion rules exactly |
| Indie Hackers | Week 1 | Share the story, not just the product |
| Niche communities (Discord, Slack, forums) | Week 1-2 | Only post where you are already a member |

**Launch checklist:**
- Working landing page with clear value proposition
- One-click signup or try-it flow (no friction)
- Way to collect feedback (email, form, or chat)
- Social proof if you have any (beta user quotes, metrics)

---

## Phase 5: After Launch

- **Respond to every piece of feedback** in the first two weeks. Early users are your best source of direction.
- **Track one metric.** Pick the single number that tells you if the project is working (daily active users, signups, messages sent). Ignore vanity metrics.
- **Ship a small improvement every week.** Momentum matters more than perfection.
- **Decide at 30 days:** Is there signal? If users are returning and giving feedback, keep going. If crickets despite promotion, either pivot the positioning or move on.

---

## Anti-Patterns

- Building for 6 months without showing anyone
- Adding features instead of talking to users
- Waiting until it is "ready" (it never will be)
- Comparing your day-one launch to someone else's year-three product
- Giving up after a quiet launch day (most products need multiple launch pushes)
