## Core Concepts

Competitive selling is the practice of positioning your product against alternatives in a way that highlights genuine differentiation without resorting to fear, uncertainty, or dishonesty. The best competitive sellers do not attack competitors; they reframe the evaluation criteria in their favor.

### Reframing Over Bashing

The cardinal rule: never trash a competitor. Prospects distrust vendors who attack each other, and it often backfires by making the competitor seem more relevant. Instead, reframe the conversation around criteria where you are strongest.

Example: if a competitor has a stronger feature in area X, do not say "their X is unreliable." Instead, ask the prospect: "How important is Y to your evaluation?" where Y is your strength. This shifts the scorecard without mentioning the competitor.

### The Competitive Intelligence Stack

Outline's collaborative knowledge base model is ideal for maintaining competitive intelligence because it supports real-time updates, cross-linking, and team contributions. Build your competitive stack with these living documents:

1. **Competitor profiles** - One page per competitor covering their positioning, ideal customer, pricing model, recent product changes, and funding status. Update monthly.
2. **Battle cards** - One-page tactical references for reps (see SKL-0212 for structure). Update quarterly or when a major competitor release ships.
3. **Feature comparison matrix** - A factual, regularly audited grid comparing capabilities. Never exaggerate your features or understate theirs; reps will lose credibility if a prospect fact-checks.
4. **Win/loss stories** - Real deal narratives (anonymized) showing what worked and what did not against each competitor. Stories are more memorable than bullet points.

### The Trap-Setting Technique

Instead of reacting to competitor claims, proactively set "landmine questions" that expose competitor weaknesses naturally:

- "Have you asked [Competitor] about their approach to [area of weakness]?"
- "When you evaluated [Competitor], did they show you [capability they lack]?"
- "What happens in [Competitor] when you need to [thing they handle poorly]?"

These questions plant doubt without making direct claims, letting the prospect discover the limitation themselves.

### Win/Loss Analysis for Competitive Learning

After every competitive deal (won or lost), conduct a brief analysis:
- **Who was in the deal?** Which competitors, and at what stage did they enter?
- **What were the deciding criteria?** Price, features, trust, timeline?
- **What did the prospect say about us vs. them?** Direct quotes are gold.
- **What would we do differently?** Tactical lessons for the next deal.

Aggregate these across 20-30 deals and patterns emerge: you may discover you win on implementation speed but lose on enterprise compliance features, for example.

### Staying Current

Competitive intelligence decays fast. Assign a "competitive champion" per major competitor - someone responsible for monitoring their changelog, blog, and G2/Capterra reviews monthly. Pipe updates into the knowledge base so the whole team benefits.

## Key Takeaways

- Never bash competitors; reframe evaluation criteria toward your strengths instead.
- Maintain living competitive documents in a collaborative knowledge base, not static PDFs.
- Use landmine questions to let prospects discover competitor weaknesses on their own.
- Conduct win/loss analysis on every competitive deal and aggregate patterns over time.
- Assign competitive champions to keep intelligence current; stale battle cards are worse than none.
