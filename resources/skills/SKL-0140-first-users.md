## Purpose

The hardest users to get are the first ones. This fragment covers practical strategies for finding your first 10, 100, and 1,000 users, collecting feedback that actually helps, and recognizing whether you have something worth continuing.

---

## Before You Start: Validate Demand

One of the most common mistakes is building a product without validating demand first. Before spending time on acquisition:

1. **Can you describe who this is for in one sentence?** "Freelance designers who need to send invoices" not "everyone who needs to get paid."
2. **Have you talked to 5 potential users?** Ask what they currently use, what frustrates them, and how they found their current solution.
3. **Define your Ideal Customer Profile.** Who are your best-fit users? What pain points do they share? Where do they hang out online?

---

## The First 10 Users: Manual and Personal

Your first users will not come from marketing. They come from personal effort.

- **Your network.** Friends, colleagues, online connections who fit the profile. Ask them directly: "I built X, would you try it and give me honest feedback?"
- **One-on-one outreach.** Find people on Twitter, LinkedIn, or forums who have publicly described the problem you solve. Send a short, personal message. Not a pitch. A conversation.
- **Existing communities you belong to.** Share what you built in communities where you are already known and trusted. Authenticity is the only currency here.

Do not scale outreach at this stage. You want conversations, not clicks.

---

## The First 100 Users: Platforms and Content

| Channel | Approach | Key Rule |
|---------|----------|----------|
| **Reddit** | Find subreddits where your users discuss the problem. Participate genuinely before posting. | Redditors detect marketing instantly. Be real or do not post. |
| **Product Hunt** | Prepare assets, recruit early supporters, engage with every comment. | One shot at a good launch. Prepare for a week, launch on Tuesday-Thursday. |
| **Hacker News (Show HN)** | Write a factual title. The best Show HN posts explain what you built and why. | Substance wins. Hype loses. |
| **Indie Hackers** | Share your building story, not just your product. | The community values transparency and numbers. |
| **Niche forums and Discords** | Only post where you are a genuine member. | If your first post is self-promotion, you will be banned or ignored. |
| **Building in public** | Share updates, learnings, and metrics on Twitter/LinkedIn. | Consistency matters more than polish. |

## Collecting Feedback That Helps

- **Ask specific questions.** "What confused you?" is better than "What do you think?"
- **Watch what they do, not just what they say.** If users say they love it but never come back, actions speak louder.
- **Talk to churned users.** People who tried it and stopped are your most valuable feedback source. Ask them why.
- **Keep a feedback log.** A simple spreadsheet: who said what, when, and what pattern is emerging.

---

## Measuring What Matters

At the early stage, track only two things:

| Metric | What It Tells You |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Activation rate** | What percentage of signups actually complete the core action? (If 100 people sign up but only 5 use it, you have an onboarding problem.) |
| **Retention** | Do users come back after day 1, day 7, day 30? Returning users are the only reliable signal of value. |

Ignore vanity metrics (page views, social media likes, total signups). They feel good but reveal nothing about product-market fit.

---

## The First 1,000 Users: Repeatable Channels

Once you have 100 users and signs of retention, look for a repeatable channel:

- **SEO content.** Write articles that answer the exact questions your users Google. This compounds over time.
- **Referrals.** If users love the product, make it easy for them to share it. A simple "invite a friend" flow.
- **Integrations.** Connect with tools your users already use. Being listed in a marketplace or plugin directory brings discovery.
- **Email newsletter.** Build a direct relationship. Algorithms change. Your email list is yours.

---

## Pivot vs. Persist

At 30-60 days after launch, ask:

- **Are users coming back without being prompted?** If yes, persist and double down.
- **Are users giving you feature requests?** If yes, they care enough to want more. Good sign.
- **Is it crickets despite multiple launch pushes?** If yes, the problem may not be marketing. Re-examine the product, the audience, or the positioning.
- **Are you solving a "nice to have" or a "need to have"?** Nice-to-haves are hard to grow. Needs grow themselves.

Pivoting is not failure. It is using real data to make a better decision.
