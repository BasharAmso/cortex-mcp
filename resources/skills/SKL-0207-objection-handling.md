## Core Concepts

An objection is not a rejection. It is a signal that the prospect needs more information, reassurance, or a different framing. The best objection handlers treat pushback as a conversation to deepen, not a wall to break through.

### The Four Objection Categories

Every objection falls into one of four buckets. Classifying quickly tells you which response framework to use:

| Category | Example | Root Cause |
|----------|---------|------------|
| **Price** | "It's too expensive" | Value not yet clear, or budget genuinely constrained |
| **Timing** | "Not right now" | No urgency, or competing priorities |
| **Authority** | "I need to check with my boss" | Not the decision-maker, or needs internal ammunition |
| **Fit** | "We already have something for this" | Differentiation unclear, or genuinely wrong fit |

### The ARC Framework

A simple three-step model for handling any objection:

1. **Acknowledge** -- Validate their concern without being defensive. "That makes sense, budget is always a factor." This mirrors Chatwoot's approach to customer conversations: start with empathy before solutions.
2. **Reframe** -- Shift the conversation from cost to value, from timing to cost-of-delay, from authority to enablement. "Most of our customers found the payback period was under 90 days."
3. **Confirm** -- Check if the reframe landed. "Does that change how you're thinking about the timeline?" If not, dig deeper rather than pushing harder.

### Building a Response Library

Just as Chatwoot provides canned responses for support teams to reply faster to frequently asked questions, sales teams should maintain a shared library of tested responses to common objections. Organize by category, include the context where each response works best, and update based on what actually closes deals. New reps can reference this library until responses become natural.

### The Hidden Objection

The stated objection is often not the real one. "Let me think about it" usually means something specific is unresolved. Use clarifying questions to surface what is actually blocking the decision: "Totally understand. When you think about it, what specifically will you be weighing?" This opens the door to address the real concern.

### When to Walk Away

Not every objection should be overcome. If the prospect genuinely lacks budget, authority, or need, pushing harder damages trust and wastes both parties' time. Qualify quickly using the objection categories above. If two or more categories show red flags, gracefully exit and revisit when circumstances change.

## Key Takeaways

- Classify every objection into price, timing, authority, or fit before responding
- Use the ARC framework: Acknowledge, Reframe, Confirm
- Build and maintain a shared response library organized by objection category
- Dig for hidden objections when the stated concern feels vague
- Walk away from deals where multiple categories show genuine disqualifiers
