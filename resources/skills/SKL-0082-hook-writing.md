# Hook Writing

The first line decides whether anyone reads the rest. A hook creates a reason to keep reading by opening a gap between what the reader knows and what they want to know. Automated prose linters like write-good flag weak openers (sentences starting with "So," filler adverbs, passive constructions) because those patterns signal a buried hook.

## Why Hooks Matter

People decide in under 2 seconds whether to keep scrolling. Your first line is not an introduction. It is an audition. Linting tools detect weasel words ("many," "various," "extremely") because they weaken opening impact. If your hook contains words a linter would flag, rewrite it.

## Core Mechanics

| Mechanic | What It Does | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Curiosity Gap | Opens a question the reader needs answered | "I almost quit my business last Tuesday." |
| Pattern Interrupt | Says something unexpected for the context | "The worst marketing advice I ever followed made me $40K." |
| Open Loop | Starts a story without finishing it | "Three months ago I sent one email that changed everything." |
| Specificity | Concrete details signal a real story | "At 2am on a Thursday, I rewrote my entire landing page." |
| Contrarian | Challenges a common belief | "You don't need a content calendar." |

## 12 Hook Templates

Use these as starting structures. Fill in your own specifics.

1. **The Confession:** "I used to [common mistake]. Here's what I do now."
2. **The Contrarian:** "Stop [popular advice]. It's costing you [consequence]."
3. **The Number:** "[Specific number] [things] I learned from [experience]."
4. **The Question:** "What would you do if [specific scenario]?"
5. **The Before/After:** "A year ago I [struggled with X]. Today I [result]."
6. **The Mistake:** "The biggest mistake I see in [topic] is [mistake]."
7. **The Observation:** "I've noticed something about [group] that nobody talks about."
8. **The Challenge:** "Try this for 7 days and tell me it doesn't work."
9. **The Hot Take:** "[Unpopular opinion] is actually [your take]."
10. **The Story Drop:** "Last week, [specific event happened]. I didn't expect what came next."
11. **The Simple Truth:** "[Topic] is simpler than you think. Here's the whole thing."
12. **The Social Proof:** "I asked [number] [people] what [question]. The answers surprised me."

## Platform Adjustments

| Platform | Hook Style | Max Length |
|----------|-----------|------------|
| LinkedIn | Professional story, contrarian insight, personal confession | ~150 chars before "see more" |
| Twitter/X | Punchy, one-line, provocative or ultra-specific | ~100 chars for max impact |
| YouTube | Curiosity + promise of payoff in title; first 5 seconds in video | Title: 60 chars, thumbnail: 5 words |
| Newsletter | Subject line = hook; first sentence = second hook | Subject: 40 chars ideal |
| Blog | SEO-aware headline + narrative opening paragraph | Headline: 60 chars for Google |

## The Hook Lint Test

Before publishing, run your hook through these checks (inspired by automated prose analysis):

1. Does it start with "So" or "There is"? (Delete those words and start with the real sentence.)
2. Does it contain adverbs like "really," "very," or "extremely"? (Replace with a specific detail.)
3. Is it in passive voice? ("A lesson was learned" vs "I learned something that changed everything.")
4. Does it contain weasel words like "many," "some," or "various"? (Use a number instead.)
5. Would you stop scrolling to read this? (Be honest.)
6. Could you cut the first sentence and start with the second? (Often, yes.)

## Common Mistakes

- Starting with context instead of tension ("In today's world..." is a cliche a linter would catch)
- Burying the hook in paragraph two (move it to line one)
- Being vague when you could be specific ("a lot of money" vs "$4,200")
- Writing a hook that promises something the content doesn't deliver
- Using wordy phrases where a direct statement works ("In order to" becomes "To")
