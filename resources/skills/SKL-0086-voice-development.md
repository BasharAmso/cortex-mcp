# Voice Development

Your voice is what makes your writing sound like you and not like anyone else. It stays constant. Tone shifts based on context. Developing voice takes intention, not talent. Prose linting tools like write-good reveal voice by showing you what to remove: strip out passive voice, weasel words, adverbs, and cliches, and what remains is closer to your natural voice than any amount of polishing.

## Voice vs Tone

| | Voice | Tone |
|---|-------|------|
| Definition | Your personality on the page | The mood of a specific piece |
| Changes? | Rarely. It's who you are. | Every piece. It fits the context. |
| Example | "Direct, warm, uses short sentences" | Encouraging (in a tutorial) vs serious (in a warning) |
| Analogy | Your personality | Your mood today |

You have one voice. You use many tones.

## The Voice Discovery Exercise

Answer these questions honestly (in your own words, not polished answers):

1. **When you explain something to a friend, how do you talk?** (Fast? Slow? Lots of examples? Blunt?)
2. **What do people compliment about your communication?** ("You make things simple" or "You're funny" or "You're direct")
3. **What writers or creators do you sound most like?** (Not who you admire. Who you actually sound like.)
4. **What would you never say?** (Jargon? Slang? Formal language? Emojis?)
5. **Read your last 5 messages to a friend.** What patterns do you see in sentence length, punctuation, and word choice?

## Voice Through Subtraction

Run your writing through the same checks a prose linter applies:

1. **Remove passive voice.** "The decision was made" vs "I decided." Active voice sounds more like a human talking.
2. **Cut weasel words.** "Many people feel" vs "Founders feel." Specificity is personality.
3. **Delete filler adverbs.** "I really believe" vs "I believe." Conviction without qualifiers is a voice choice.
4. **Replace cliches.** "Think outside the box" vs your original phrasing. Cliches are someone else's voice.
5. **Eliminate "there is/are" starts.** "There are three reasons" vs "Three reasons." Direct openers reflect a direct voice.

What survives this process is your voice. The patterns you keep after removing generic filler are the patterns that define you.

## Voice Attributes Framework

Pick 3-5 attributes that describe your writing voice. Use this format:

**[Attribute] but not [extreme]**

Examples:
- "Casual but not sloppy"
- "Confident but not arrogant"
- "Warm but not cheesy"
- "Direct but not rude"
- "Funny but not trying hard"

These guardrails define your range. Write them down and reference them before creating content.

## Voice Guidelines Template

Create a one-page reference you can share with editors, collaborators, or AI tools:

```
Voice Name: [Your Name]'s Writing Voice

Core Attributes:
- [Attribute 1] but not [extreme]
- [Attribute 2] but not [extreme]
- [Attribute 3] but not [extreme]

Sentence Style:
- Average length: [short / medium / long]
- Structure: [simple and direct / varied / complex]

Words I Use: [list 10 words you naturally reach for]
Words I Never Use: [list 10 words that don't sound like you]

Punctuation Habits: [ellipses? exclamation marks? parentheses?]

How I Open: [with a story? a question? a bold claim?]
How I Close: [with a takeaway? a question? a call to action?]

Example Paragraph (raw, unedited, in my real voice):
[Write one paragraph exactly how you'd say it out loud]
```

## Consistency Across Platforms

Your voice stays the same. The format changes.

| Platform | Adaptation | Voice Stays |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| LinkedIn | Professional context, longer form allowed | Same sentence style, same humor level |
| Twitter/X | Compressed, punchier, fewer qualifiers | Same opinions, same word choices |
| Newsletter | More personal, can go deeper | Same warmth, same directness |
| Blog | More structured, includes evidence | Same perspective, same examples |

## Voice Killers (Avoid These)

- **Over-editing:** Your fifth revision often sounds less like you than your first draft
- **Copying someone else's style:** Influence is fine; imitation kills authenticity
- **Writing for "everyone":** A voice that tries to please everyone sounds like no one
- **Removing all personality:** "Professional" does not mean "bland"
- **Letting AI fully rewrite you:** Use AI to edit, not to replace your phrasing

## The Raw Draft Test

Write your first draft fast, without editing. Then compare it to your published content. If they sound like two different people, you are over-editing. Your raw draft is usually closer to your real voice. The Elements of Style principle applies: "Omit needless words." But only the needless ones. Your quirks, your rhythm, your word choices are not needless.
