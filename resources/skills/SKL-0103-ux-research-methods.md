# UX Research Methods

Select the right research method for the right question. Different stages of product development call for different approaches. Research is not a phase; it is a continuous practice.

## Research Types by Stage

| Stage | Goal | Methods |
|-------|------|---------|
| **Discovery** | Understand the problem space | Stakeholder interviews, contextual inquiry, diary studies |
| **Foundational** | Define user needs and behaviors | User interviews, card sorting, journey mapping |
| **Generative** | Explore solution directions | Prototyping, co-design workshops, concept testing |
| **Evaluative** | Test specific solutions | Usability testing, A/B testing, multivariate testing |
| **Ongoing** | Monitor live product health | Analytics review, surveys, feedback analysis |

## Method Selection Guide

**Qualitative** (why and how): User interviews, contextual inquiry, think-aloud testing, diary studies. Best when exploring unknowns and building empathy. 5-8 participants per round is sufficient to identify major themes.

**Quantitative** (what and how much): Surveys, A/B tests, analytics, card sorting (with enough participants). Best when measuring frequency, severity, or comparing options. Need statistical significance, typically 30+ responses minimum.

## Research Planning Checklist

1. **Define research questions** using action verbs: "identify," "describe," "evaluate," not vague "understand"
2. **Select methods** matching your questions (qualitative for exploration, quantitative for measurement)
3. **Recruit participants** representing your actual user base including underserved groups
4. **Write a discussion guide** with open-ended questions, ordered from broad to specific
5. **Assign team roles**: moderator, notetaker, observer
6. **Plan analysis time**: allocate twice as long for synthesis as you spent conducting sessions

## Bias Awareness

Nine common research biases to guard against:

- **Confirmation bias**: Noticing only evidence that supports your hypothesis
- **Framing effect**: Question phrasing that leads participants toward specific answers
- **Sampling bias**: Recruiting only easily accessible participants, missing edge cases
- **Social desirability**: Participants saying what they think you want to hear
- **Survivorship bias**: Studying only successful users, ignoring those who abandoned

Mitigate by: rotating team roles, involving diverse perspectives in analysis, using open-ended questions, and distinguishing observations from interpretations.

## Making Research Actionable

Raw data becomes useful through structured synthesis:

- **Affinity diagramming**: Group observations bottom-up to discover themes
- **Journey mapping**: Visualize user steps, emotions, and pain points across touchpoints
- **Personas**: Create archetypes from observed goals, behaviors, and frustrations
- **Mental models**: Document how users think about the problem (vs how you designed the system)

Present findings as specific, evidence-backed recommendations tied to user quotes and observed behaviors, not abstract summaries.
