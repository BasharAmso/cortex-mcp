## Core Concepts

Thought leadership is not self-promotion with a fancier label. Ghost's publishing ecosystem is built by creators who earned authority through consistent, specific, experience-backed content. The pattern across successful Ghost publishers is the same: they share what they actually do, challenge conventional wisdom with evidence, and give away their best thinking for free.

### The Authority Ladder

Thought leadership develops in stages. You cannot skip levels:

1. **Practitioner** — You do the work and share what you learn (blog posts, how-tos)
2. **Synthesizer** — You connect patterns across experiences and name them (frameworks, models)
3. **Challenger** — You question industry consensus with evidence from your practice
4. **Shaper** — Your ideas influence how others think about the domain

Most people stall at level 1 because they only share tactics. Moving to level 2 requires naming the patterns you see.

### Developing Original Takes

Original thinking comes from a repeatable process, not sudden inspiration:

1. **Notice friction.** When conventional advice does not match your experience, write about why.
2. **Name the pattern.** Give your observation a label. "The Expertise Paradox" is more memorable than "experts sometimes struggle with simple tasks."
3. **Provide evidence.** Your own experience, data, or case studies. No borrowed authority from quoting famous people.
4. **Acknowledge the counter.** Steel-man the opposing view before explaining why your take differs. This builds credibility.
5. **Make it actionable.** End with what the reader should do differently based on your insight.

### Contrarian Thinking Framework

Not every post needs to be contrarian, but strategic disagreement accelerates authority:

| Type | Example | Risk Level |
|------|---------|------------|
| **Challenge a best practice** | "Code reviews are slowing your team down" | Medium |
| **Reframe a problem** | "You don't have a hiring problem, you have a retention problem" | Low |
| **Predict a shift** | "No-code will replace 40% of junior dev roles by 2028" | High |
| **Reveal a hidden cost** | "The real cost of microservices nobody talks about" | Low |

Rules for contrarian content: (1) Only disagree with things you have direct experience with. (2) Attack ideas, not people. (3) Offer a better alternative, not just criticism.

### Content Types for Authority Building

| Content Type | Authority Signal | Frequency |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Original frameworks | "This person thinks differently" | Monthly |
| Experience-based stories | "This person has done the work" | Weekly |
| Industry analysis | "This person sees the big picture" | Bi-weekly |
| Tactical how-tos | "This person is generous with knowledge" | Weekly |
| Curated commentary | "This person is well-read and connected" | As needed |

### Common Traps

- **The echo chamber** — Restating what everyone already agrees with adds zero authority
- **The credential flex** — Leading with titles instead of insights signals insecurity
- **The generalist trap** — Being known for everything means being known for nothing
- **The theory trap** — Sharing frameworks without showing your own results rings hollow

## Key Takeaways

- Thought leadership is earned through stages: practitioner, synthesizer, challenger, shaper
- Original takes come from noticing where conventional advice fails in practice
- Name your patterns and frameworks to make ideas memorable and shareable
- Contrarian content accelerates authority but must be grounded in direct experience
- Specificity beats breadth; own a narrow domain deeply before expanding
