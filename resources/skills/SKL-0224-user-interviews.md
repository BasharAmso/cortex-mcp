## Core Concepts

User interviews are the foundation of qualitative market research. The goal is to uncover real problems, not validate your assumptions. Formbricks demonstrates this principle by offering multiple survey delivery channels (in-app, website, link, email) because different contexts yield different quality of feedback. Apply the same thinking to interviews: where and how you ask matters as much as what you ask.

### The Interview Framework

**1. Preparation (before the call)**
- Define your research question: one specific thing you want to learn.
- Recruit 5-8 participants per segment. More than 8 produces diminishing returns for discovery research.
- Write an interview guide, not a script. List 8-12 open-ended questions grouped by theme.

**2. Question Design (the TEDW framework)**
- **Tell me about...** — Opens with their experience, not your product. "Tell me about the last time you tried to solve X."
- **Example** — Ask for specific instances. "Can you walk me through a recent example?"
- **Difficulty** — Probe for pain. "What was the hardest part of that?"
- **Workaround** — Reveal current solutions. "How do you handle that today?"

Avoid leading questions ("Don't you think X is frustrating?") and hypothetical futures ("Would you use a tool that...?"). People cannot reliably predict their own behavior.

**3. Active Listening Techniques**
- Use the 80/20 rule: participant talks 80% of the time.
- Follow up with "Why?" at least twice to get past surface answers.
- Mirror their exact language back to them rather than paraphrasing into your terminology.

**4. Synthesis**
- Tag each insight with a theme (pain point, current behavior, desired outcome, objection).
- Look for patterns across 3+ participants. A single mention is an anecdote; three mentions is a signal.
- Create an insight statement: "[User type] struggles with [problem] because [root cause], and currently handles it by [workaround]."

### Targeting the Right Users

Formbricks enables targeting surveys to specific user groups without code changes, using behavioral and demographic filters. Apply the same rigor to interview recruitment: screen for participants who have recently experienced the problem (within 2-4 weeks), not those who might theoretically care about it.

## Key Takeaways

- Ask about past behavior, never hypothetical futures. "Tell me about the last time..." beats "Would you ever...?"
- Recruit for recency of the problem, not general interest in your category.
- Synthesize across participants by tagging themes; 3+ mentions of the same pain point is a validated signal.
- Use the TEDW framework (Tell, Example, Difficulty, Workaround) to structure questions without scripting the conversation.
- Keep interviews to 30-45 minutes. Longer sessions produce fatigue, not deeper insights.
