## Core Concepts

Developer career growth is not about learning more frameworks. It is about expanding the scope of problems you can solve and the impact you create. The most common growth framework maps this as expanding influence radius.

### Industry Leveling Framework

Most tech companies use a leveling system that maps to these general tiers:

| Level | Title | Scope | Key Shift |
|-------|-------|-------|-----------|
| L1-L2 | Junior | Tasks within a feature | Learning to write production code |
| L3 | Mid-level | Full features end-to-end | Owning delivery without hand-holding |
| L4 | Senior | Cross-feature, team impact | Influencing technical direction |
| L5 | Staff | Cross-team, org impact | Solving ambiguous problems, force multiplier |
| L6+ | Principal/Fellow | Company or industry impact | Setting technical vision, industry thought leadership |

The developer-roadmap project maps the technical skills expected at each level, but technical skill alone stops being the primary growth lever after senior level. Beyond L4, impact, communication, and judgment matter more than code output.

### IC vs Management Decision

This is not a binary choice but a spectrum. Consider these honest questions:

**Stay IC if you:**
- Get energy from solving deep technical problems
- Want to grow influence through expertise, not authority
- Prefer individual focus time over meeting-heavy days
- Are willing to accept that IC tracks have lower ceilings at many companies

**Consider management if you:**
- Get energy from developing other people
- Want to shape team culture and processes
- Are comfortable with ambiguous, people-oriented problems
- Accept that you will write less code (eventually almost none)

Many companies now offer Staff+ IC tracks with compensation parity to management. Ask about this before assuming management is the only path to higher compensation.

### The Skills Matrix Beyond Code

| Skill Area | Junior → Mid | Mid → Senior | Senior → Staff |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Technical** | Write clean code | Design systems | Set technical direction |
| **Communication** | Document your work | Explain trade-offs to non-engineers | Write influential proposals |
| **Ownership** | Complete assigned tasks | Own a feature end-to-end | Own a problem space |
| **Collaboration** | Work on a team | Unblock teammates | Align multiple teams |
| **Judgment** | Follow best practices | Choose the right trade-offs | Know when to break the rules |

### Promotion Strategy

Promotions reward demonstrated behavior, not promised potential:

1. **Understand the rubric.** Get the actual leveling criteria from your manager or HR. Do not guess what "senior" means at your company.
2. **Operate at the next level.** Consistently demonstrate next-level behaviors for 3-6 months before the promotion conversation. The promotion should feel like a formality.
3. **Make your work visible.** Write design docs, present at team meetings, send summary emails of project outcomes. Invisible work does not get rewarded.
4. **Build sponsors, not just advocates.** A sponsor is someone senior who will argue for your promotion in calibration meetings when you are not in the room.
5. **Keep a brag document.** Record accomplishments weekly: projects shipped, problems solved, people helped, metrics improved. Use this in promotion discussions.

### Career Transitions

Common developer career transitions:
- **Specialist → Generalist**: Broaden your stack. Good for early career.
- **Generalist → Specialist**: Deepen in a domain (ML, security, infrastructure). Good for mid-career differentiation.
- **Engineering → Product**: Leverage technical understanding to shape product decisions.
- **IC → Management → IC**: The "management tour" builds empathy and organizational skills even if you return to IC.

## Key Takeaways

- Career growth after senior level depends more on scope of impact than technical skill
- IC and management are both valid paths; choose based on what gives you energy, not what seems prestigious
- Promotions reward demonstrated behavior, not promises: operate at the next level before asking
- Keep a brag document and make your work visible to build sponsorship
- The skills matrix shifts from code quality (junior) to technical judgment and influence (staff+)
