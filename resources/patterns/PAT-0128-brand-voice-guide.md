## Core Concepts

Your brand voice is how you sound in writing. It is the personality that comes through in every blog post, email, social media update, and presentation. Without a documented voice guide, your communication drifts over time, especially if multiple people create content or if you use AI tools to assist with writing. A voice guide is a reference document that keeps your communication recognizable and consistent.

### Voice vs. Tone

Voice is your consistent personality. It does not change. Tone is how that personality adapts to context. Think of it like a person: your friend has a consistent personality (voice), but they speak differently at a funeral than at a birthday party (tone). Outline, the open-source knowledge base, demonstrates this distinction in their own documentation: their voice is always clear, direct, and developer-friendly, but their tone shifts between casual in blog posts and precise in API documentation.

### Defining Your Voice in Three Attributes

Pick three adjectives that describe how you want to sound. These become your voice pillars. Examples:

- **Warm, direct, practical** (mentor-like professional)
- **Sharp, curious, playful** (industry commentator)
- **Calm, authoritative, generous** (domain expert)

For each attribute, write one sentence explaining what it means in practice. Then write one example sentence that demonstrates it and one anti-example that violates it. This gives collaborators and AI tools concrete guidance, not abstract descriptions.

### The Tone Matrix

Create a simple matrix mapping communication contexts to tone adjustments:

| Context | Tone Shift | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| LinkedIn post | Conversational, encouraging | "Here's what I learned from shipping the wrong feature." |
| Client email | Professional, warm | "Thanks for flagging this. Here's what we're doing about it." |
| Technical blog | Clear, teaching | "This pattern solves X. Here's how to implement it step by step." |
| Error message | Empathetic, actionable | "Something went wrong. Try refreshing, or contact support if it persists." |
| Conference talk | Energetic, storytelling | "Three months ago, I was convinced this would never work." |

Outline's documentation system supports this kind of structured reference well: nested documents, templates, and collaborative editing make it easy to maintain a living voice guide that multiple people can access.

### Do and Don't Examples

The most useful part of any voice guide is concrete examples. For each voice pillar, list three to five writing patterns you follow and three to five you avoid:

**Do:**
- Use first person ("I" and "we") to create connection
- Ask questions to engage the reader
- Share specific numbers and examples instead of vague claims
- Write short sentences and short paragraphs

**Don't:**
- Use jargon without explanation
- Write in passive voice ("mistakes were made")
- Use filler phrases ("in order to," "it is important to note that")
- Over-qualify statements ("I think maybe possibly this could work")

### Maintaining the Guide

A voice guide is a living document. Review it every six months. As your brand evolves, your voice may sharpen or shift. Update the guide when you notice new patterns in your best-performing content or when collaborators consistently misinterpret your voice. Outline's version history and collaborative editing features make this kind of iterative documentation natural: track changes, leave comments, and keep a single source of truth.

## Key Takeaways

- Voice is your consistent personality; tone is how it adapts to context. Define both.
- Choose three voice attributes and document each with examples and anti-examples.
- Create a tone matrix mapping different contexts to specific tone adjustments.
- Include concrete do and don't examples. These are the most actionable part of any voice guide.
- Treat the guide as a living document. Review and update every six months.
