## Purpose

Developers are a unique audience. Traditional marketing tactics (ads, sales calls, hype copy) actively repel them. This fragment covers how to reach developers authentically through documentation, content, community, and open source.

---

## Core Principle: Value First, Product Second

Developers adopt tools that solve real problems and are easy to evaluate. Every marketing action should either teach something useful or remove friction from trying your product.

---

## Documentation as Marketing

Your docs are your best marketing asset. Developers evaluate tools by reading documentation before anything else.

- **Quick start that works in under 5 minutes.** If a developer cannot get a "hello world" running fast, they leave.
- **Real examples, not abstract descriptions.** Show working code for common use cases.
- **SEO happens naturally.** Documentation pages that answer specific questions rank well. "How to do X with Y" pages attract organic search traffic.
- **Keep docs current.** Outdated documentation is worse than no documentation. It signals an abandoned project.

---

## Content Strategy

| Content Type | Purpose | Frequency |
|-------------|---------|-----------|
| **Tutorials** | Show how to solve real problems using your tool | 2-4 per month |
| **Blog posts** | Share technical insights, architecture decisions, lessons learned | 1-2 per month |
| **Email newsletter** | Direct relationship with your audience, not algorithm-dependent | Weekly or biweekly |
| **Changelog** | Show momentum and responsiveness | Every release |

**Repurpose everything.** One tutorial becomes a blog post, a Twitter thread, a conference talk, and a newsletter issue. Different formats reach different people.

---

## Launch Platforms for Developer Products

| Platform | What Works | What Fails |
|----------|-----------|------------|
| **Hacker News (Show HN)** | Factual title, genuine technical substance, engage in comments | Hype language, marketing speak, ignoring feedback |
| **Product Hunt** | Clean visuals, clear one-liner, respond to every comment | Launching without a network to upvote early |
| **Reddit (r/programming, niche subs)** | Genuine contribution, respecting community rules | Obvious self-promotion, posting without participating |
| **Dev.to / Hashnode** | Technical tutorials with real code | Thinly disguised product pitches |

**Launch timing:** Tuesday through Thursday sees highest engagement. Avoid Mondays. Spend as much time distributing content as creating it.

---

## Open Source as Distribution

Open source is the most powerful distribution channel for developer tools.

- **Free tools build trust.** Developers try open source tools without a sales conversation.
- **Community projects drive awareness.** A useful free tool (even tangentially related to your product) can drive 30%+ of signups through organic referrals.
- **Contributions create advocates.** Contributors become emotionally invested in your product's success.

Characteristics of effective community tools: no signup required, solves one focused problem, completely free.

---

## Community Building

- **Developers influence other developers.** Word of mouth is the primary distribution channel. One respected developer endorsing your tool is worth more than any ad campaign.
- **Be present where they are.** Discord servers, GitHub discussions, Stack Overflow, Twitter/X, niche Slack groups.
- **Share wins and losses.** Transparency about what worked and what failed builds credibility faster than polished case studies.
- **Guest content.** Write for blogs and podcasts in your product's ecosystem. Focus on being genuinely useful to that community.

---

## Anti-Patterns

- Using enterprise sales tactics (cold emails, gated content, mandatory demos)
- Writing marketing copy instead of technical content
- Ignoring feedback on launch platforms
- Treating docs as an afterthought instead of the primary experience
- Buying ads before you have organic traction and product-market fit
