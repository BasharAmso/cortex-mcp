## Core Concepts

Focus groups gather 6-10 participants for a guided discussion that reveals attitudes, perceptions, and reasoning that individual surveys miss. The value lies in group interaction: participants build on, challenge, and refine each other's views, surfacing insights no individual interview would produce.

### When Focus Groups Work (and When They Do Not)

| Use Focus Groups For | Do Not Use Focus Groups For |
|---------------------|---------------------------|
| Exploring "why" behind behaviors | Measuring "how many" (use surveys) |
| Testing messaging and positioning | Usability testing (use 1:1 sessions) |
| Understanding language and framing | Sensitive or taboo topics (use anonymous surveys) |
| Generating hypotheses for quant research | Making statistical claims |
| Evaluating concepts and early prototypes | Final go/no-go decisions |

### Planning a Focus Group

**1. Define the research question.** Be specific. "What do developers think about our product?" is too broad. "How do mid-level developers evaluate and choose API documentation tools?" is actionable.

**2. Recruit participants.** Screen for your target segment. Aim for 6-8 participants per group (fewer creates pressure; more limits airtime). Run 3-4 groups minimum to see patterns. Use screening surveys (Formbricks-style targeted surveys work well for pre-screening) to filter for relevant experience and demographics.

**3. Build a discussion guide.** Structure the 60-90 minute session:
- **Opening (10 min):** Ground rules, introductions, warm-up question.
- **Core discussion (40-60 min):** 5-7 open-ended questions progressing from general to specific.
- **Stimulus reaction (15 min):** Show concepts, prototypes, or competitor materials. Observe immediate reactions before guided discussion.
- **Closing (5 min):** "What is the one thing we should remember from today?"

**4. Set the environment.** Comfortable, neutral space. Circular seating. Recording equipment (with consent). Remote focus groups via video call work but require tighter moderation.

### Moderation Techniques

**The funnel approach:** Start broad ("Tell me about a recent time you needed to...") and narrow progressively ("What specifically frustrated you about that experience?").

**Managing dominant participants:** Use direct redirection: "Thank you, Alex. Sarah, I noticed you were nodding. What is your perspective?" Never let one voice dominate.

**The 5-second rule:** After asking a question, wait at least 5 seconds before following up. Silence feels uncomfortable but produces deeper responses.

**Probe, do not lead:** Say "Tell me more about that" instead of "So you found it frustrating?" Leading questions produce the answers you plant.

**Track energy:** When energy drops, introduce a stimulus or activity. When energy spikes, let the conversation flow before redirecting.

### Analyzing Focus Group Data

1. **Debrief immediately** after each session while observations are fresh.
2. **Transcribe and code** responses using the interview analysis pattern (see PAT-0121).
3. **Look for patterns across groups**, not within a single group. An insight from one group is an anecdote; the same insight across three groups is a finding.
4. **Note disagreements.** Where participants argue or disagree is often where the most valuable insight lives.
5. **Pair with quantitative follow-up.** Use focus group themes to design a survey that tests hypotheses at scale.

### Common Pitfalls

- Running only one group and treating results as representative.
- Allowing groupthink to dominate (one confident voice shaping all responses).
- Over-interpreting individual quotes as market truths.
- Skipping the screening step, resulting in participants outside your target segment.

## Key Takeaways

- Focus groups reveal "why" through group dynamics; use them for exploration, not measurement
- Run 3-4 groups minimum; patterns across groups are findings, single-group insights are anecdotes
- Moderate with open-ended questions, the funnel approach, and the 5-second silence rule
- Manage dominant participants actively to prevent groupthink from corrupting data
- Always follow up qualitative focus group findings with quantitative validation
