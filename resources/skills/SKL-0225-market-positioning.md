## Core Concepts

Positioning is not what you say about your product. It is the mental space you occupy in your customer's mind relative to alternatives. PostHog demonstrates strong positioning by declaring itself an "all-in-one developer platform" rather than competing in any single analytics category. That deliberate category creation is a positioning choice.

### The Positioning Canvas

Work through these five elements in order. Each one constrains the next:

**1. Competitive Alternatives** — What would customers use if your product didn't exist? Not just direct competitors, but also spreadsheets, manual processes, or "do nothing." PostHog's alternatives include Google Analytics, Amplitude, LaunchDarkly, and Sentry, each covering one slice of what PostHog bundles.

**2. Unique Capabilities** — What can you do that alternatives cannot? List concrete features, not vague claims. PostHog's unique capability is combining product analytics, session replay, feature flags, experiments, and surveys in a single platform with a shared data model.

**3. Value Delivered** — Translate capabilities into customer outcomes. "Single platform" becomes "no data silos, one bill, correlated insights." Always express value from the customer's perspective.

**4. Target Segment** — Who cares most about that value? PostHog targets developers and technical product teams who are frustrated by stitching together 5+ separate tools. Your best segment is the group where your unique value matters most.

**5. Market Category** — The frame of reference that makes your value obvious. PostHog chose "product OS" rather than "analytics tool." The right category makes your strengths self-evident.

### Building a Positioning Map

A positioning map plots competitors on two axes that matter to your target segment. Choose axes where:
- You can credibly claim a strong position on at least one axis.
- The axes represent real decision criteria (not vanity dimensions).
- There is an underserved quadrant you can own.

Example axes for developer tools: "all-in-one vs. best-of-breed" and "self-serve vs. sales-led."

### Messaging Hierarchy

Once positioning is set, build a three-layer messaging hierarchy:
1. **Category line** — What you are (1 sentence). "The all-in-one platform for building better products."
2. **Value pillars** — 3-4 key benefits, each supported by a specific capability.
3. **Proof points** — Social proof, metrics, or demos that make each pillar believable.

Every piece of marketing copy should trace back to this hierarchy. If a message doesn't connect to a value pillar, it dilutes positioning.

## Key Takeaways

- Positioning starts with competitive alternatives, not your feature list. Understand what you're replacing.
- Unique capabilities must be concrete and demonstrable, not aspirational adjectives.
- Choose a market category that makes your strengths obvious. Creating a new category is valid if existing ones box you in.
- A positioning map needs axes that reflect real buyer decision criteria, not dimensions you invented to look good.
- The messaging hierarchy (category line, value pillars, proof points) translates positioning into usable marketing copy.
