# Headline Craft

The headline is the ad for your content. Eight out of ten people read the headline. Two out of ten read the rest. A better headline changes that ratio.

## The Six Headline Types

### 1. Number Headlines

Use a specific number to promise a concrete, scannable payoff.

- "7 Mistakes New Freelancers Make in Their First Month"
- "3 Questions That Changed How I Think About Pricing"

**Why they work:** Numbers set expectations. The reader knows what they're getting.

**Tips:** Odd numbers outperform even. Small numbers (3-9) work for advice. Larger numbers (21, 50) work for lists and resources.

### 2. How-To Headlines

Promise a skill transfer. The reader will learn to do something.

- "How to Write a LinkedIn Post in 15 Minutes"
- "How to Price Your Services Without Undercharging"

**Why they work:** Direct promise of practical value.

**Tips:** Add a constraint (time, difficulty, tool) to make it specific: "How to X Without Y" or "How to X in [timeframe]."

### 3. Curiosity Headlines

Open a question the reader needs to answer.

- "The Writing Advice I Wish I'd Ignored Sooner"
- "Why Most Content Strategies Fail by Month Three"

**Why they work:** The brain wants closure. An unanswered question creates tension.

**Tips:** Don't be vague. "This One Trick" is clickbait. "The Pricing Mistake That Cost Me $8K" is curiosity with specificity.

### 4. Benefit-Driven Headlines

Lead with the outcome the reader wants.

- "Write Faster Without Sacrificing Quality"
- "Get More Replies to Your Cold Emails"

**Why they work:** They answer "what's in it for me?" immediately.

**Tips:** Name the benefit in the reader's language, not yours. "Increase engagement" is your language. "Get more comments" is theirs.

### 5. Negative/Warning Headlines

Promise to help them avoid a mistake.

- "Stop Making These 5 Mistakes on Your About Page"
- "Why Your Newsletter Isn't Growing (And How to Fix It)"

**Why they work:** Loss aversion. People work harder to avoid pain than to gain pleasure.

### 6. Question Headlines

Ask something the reader wants answered.

- "Is Your Landing Page Costing You Customers?"
- "What Happens When You Post Every Day for 30 Days?"

**Why they work:** Questions engage the reader's brain actively.

**Tips:** Only ask questions where the reader doesn't already know the answer.

## Platform-Specific Length Guide

| Platform | Ideal Length | Why |
|----------|-------------|-----|
| Google (SEO) | 50-60 characters | Truncates after ~60 in search results |
| LinkedIn article | 40-70 characters | Shorter = more clicks in feed |
| Email subject line | 30-50 characters | Mobile preview cuts at ~40 |
| YouTube | 50-70 characters | Must work as both text and thumbnail complement |
| Twitter/X | Under 100 characters | Leave room for engagement |
| Blog/Newsletter | 60-80 characters | Enough space to be specific |

## The Headline Testing Method

Never publish your first headline. Write at least 5 variations using different types, then pick the best one.

**Process:**

1. Write your draft headline
2. Rewrite it as a number headline
3. Rewrite it as a how-to
4. Rewrite it as a curiosity headline
5. Rewrite it as a benefit headline
6. Read all five aloud. Which one would make you click?

**Example set:**
- Draft: "Writing Better Content"
- Number: "5 Changes That Made My Content Twice as Engaging"
- How-to: "How to Write Content People Actually Finish Reading"
- Curiosity: "The Reason Most Content Gets Ignored After the First Paragraph"
- Benefit: "Write Posts That Get Saved, Shared, and Commented On"

The draft is always the weakest. The variations always reveal a better angle.

## Headline Strength Checklist

- [ ] Is it specific? (No vague words like "better" or "great")
- [ ] Does it promise a clear benefit or spark curiosity?
- [ ] Would I click this if someone else wrote it?
- [ ] Is it the right length for the platform?
- [ ] Does the content actually deliver what the headline promises?
- [ ] Have I written at least 3 alternatives before choosing?
