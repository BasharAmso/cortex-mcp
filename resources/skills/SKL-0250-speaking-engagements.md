## Core Concepts

Speaking at events is one of the fastest ways to build authority and expand your network. A single well-received talk can generate more visibility than months of online content. But most professionals never speak because they believe they need to be invited. The reality is that most conferences actively seek speakers through open calls for proposals, and meetups are often desperate for presenters.

### The Speaking Ladder

Start small and build up:

1. **Internal presentations**: Team demos, brown bags, all-hands talks at your company.
2. **Local meetups**: 15 to 20 minute talks at community meetups. Low pressure, friendly audiences.
3. **Virtual events**: Webinars, Twitter/X Spaces, podcast guest appearances. No travel required.
4. **Regional conferences**: One to two day events with 100 to 500 attendees. Competitive CFPs but achievable.
5. **Major conferences**: Large industry events. Most speakers at this level built their reputation at stages 1 through 4 first.

Each level builds your confidence, refines your content, and adds to your speaking portfolio.

### Writing a Winning CFP

Call for Proposals (CFP) submissions are evaluated on three criteria: relevance to the audience, clarity of what attendees will learn, and evidence that you can deliver. Structure your submission with: a specific title (not vague), a two-sentence abstract that promises a clear takeaway, a three to five bullet outline of what you will cover, and a short bio proving relevant experience. Submit to five to ten conferences per talk idea. Acceptance rates at major conferences range from 5% to 20%, so volume matters.

### Talk Preparation

A 30-minute talk requires roughly 20 to 30 hours of preparation for a new speaker. Start with your outline, not your slides. Write the narrative arc first (see SKL-0247 for story structure). Then rehearse the talk out loud three to five times before building any slides. This prevents the common mistake of designing slides first and then trying to fit a narrative around them.

### Slide Design Principles

Reveal.js, the open-source HTML presentation framework, embodies the principles of effective slide design: full-screen visuals, minimal text, and progressive disclosure through nested slides and fragment animations. Apply these principles regardless of your tool:

- **One idea per slide.** If a slide has more than one concept, split it.
- **Minimal text.** Six words maximum per slide. Your voice carries the content, not the screen.
- **High-contrast visuals.** Dark backgrounds with light text or light backgrounds with dark text. Avoid low-contrast color combinations.
- **Progressive reveal.** Show information step by step rather than all at once. Reveal.js fragments and auto-animate features exist because this technique dramatically improves comprehension.

### Event Types and Strategy

Not all speaking is equal for brand building. Prioritize events where your target audience gathers, not the largest stage available. A 50-person niche meetup where every attendee is a potential client or collaborator often delivers more value than a 5,000-person conference where you are one of 200 speakers. Track which events generate follow-up conversations and repeat invitations.

## Key Takeaways

- Start with internal talks and local meetups. Build your speaking portfolio before targeting major conferences.
- Write CFP submissions focused on specific audience takeaways and submit to multiple events per talk idea.
- Prepare the narrative arc first, rehearse out loud, and build slides last.
- Follow reveal.js design principles: one idea per slide, minimal text, high contrast, progressive reveal.
- Prioritize events where your target audience gathers over events with the biggest stage.
